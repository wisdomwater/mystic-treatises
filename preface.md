## Preface

There are some books we seek because we already know what they contain.

And then there are books that seem to find us.

That was my experience with Isaac of Nineveh.

I first encountered his name through recommendations from others who had walked the contemplative path before me. They spoke of him as one of Christianity's great spiritual guides—a man whose writings possessed an unusual depth, gentleness, and wisdom. Wanting to learn from that tradition, I picked up *Mystic Treatises*, not knowing exactly what I would find.

I quickly discovered that Isaac was not interested in winning arguments or constructing theological systems. His concern was the transformation of the human heart.

Again and again he returned to two virtues that seem to stand at the center of the Christian life: mercy and humility.

Neither comes naturally to me.

Like most people, I find there is something within me that continually wants to place itself at the center of reality. Even when I desire to do good, there is often a quiet longing to be noticed, appreciated, understood, or proven right. The self has an astonishing ability to make itself the subject of every story.

Humiliation is not the answer, for humiliation often keeps us just as absorbed with ourselves as pride does. We remain the center of attention, only now in a different role.

Humility is something altogether different.

Humility is the gentle fading of the self until our attention is no longer occupied with ourselves at all. Our gaze turns instead toward God. The heart ceases its endless measuring and comparing and simply delights in the Beloved. In Isaac's vision, humility is not self-hatred but self-forgetfulness born of love. It is the freedom to stop defending ourselves because we have become captivated by the One who loved us first.

Isaac himself lived during the seventh century. Born in the region of Qatar, he eventually became a monk within the Church of the East, a Christian tradition that flourished far beyond the borders of the Byzantine Empire. For a brief time he served as Bishop of Nineveh, in what is now northern Iraq, but he soon resigned that office in order to return to the hidden life of solitude and prayer. There he devoted himself to contemplation, writing, and the patient work of seeking God.

His influence eventually spread far beyond his own tradition. His writings were translated into Greek, Syriac, Arabic, Georgian, Slavonic, and many other languages. Today he is read with deep affection by Eastern Orthodox, Oriental Orthodox, Roman Catholic, Anglican, Protestant, and many other Christians. Though he lived centuries before many of our present divisions, his voice has continued to cross them.

As I spent time with *Mystic Treatises*, I found myself wanting to slow the pace of Isaac's writing without diminishing its depth. His original work is rich, repetitive, and beautifully layered, but it was written for readers formed by a very different world. The purpose of this book is not to replace Isaac's words, but to allow modern readers to hear his voice with fresh clarity.

Each devotional focuses on a single movement from his writings. Rather than explaining Isaac, I have tried to let him speak directly to us in contemporary language while remaining faithful to his spirit. My hope is that these readings retain the spaciousness, gentleness, and quiet fire that have nourished Christians for well over a thousand years.

If these pages accomplish anything, I hope they awaken in us the same longing that first drew me to Isaac—not merely to become more knowledgeable, but to become more merciful; not merely to become more disciplined, but more humble; not merely to think about God, but to become so captivated by His love that, little by little, the self recedes and the Beloved fills our vision.

May these ancient words become a living invitation.

May they lead us into stillness.

May they teach us mercy.

And above all, may they help us discover the freedom that is found only in humility.
